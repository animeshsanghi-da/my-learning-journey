# Business Intelligence: Tableau Standard Visualizations

## Overview
Tableau is renowned for its intuitive drag-and-drop interface, allowing analysts to quickly translate raw data into powerful visual insights. Building standard visualizations—such as bar charts, line graphs, scatter plots, and maps—forms the core of the **Process** and **Analyze** phases in data storytelling. 

This guide covers how to build the most critical standard visualizations in Tableau Public, including the theoretical best practices and the exact steps to create them.

---

## 1. The "Show Me" Feature vs. Manual Building
While Tableau provides a **"Show Me"** menu in the top right corner that automatically suggests charts based on selected fields, building visualizations manually via the **Columns** and **Rows** shelves gives you maximum control over your data.

* **Columns Shelf:** Creates the X-axis (vertical dividers).
* **Rows Shelf:** Creates the Y-axis (horizontal dividers).
* **Marks Card:** Controls the visual properties of the data points (Color, Size, Label, Detail, Tooltip).

---

## 2. Bar Charts (Comparing Categories)
Bar charts are the gold standard for comparing quantitative data across different categories.

### Theoretical Best Practices:
* **Sort your data:** Always sort bar charts in descending or ascending order unless the categories have a natural chronological order (e.g., days of the week).
* **Horizontal vs. Vertical:** Use horizontal bar charts when category labels are long, preventing text from overlapping or becoming hard to read.

### How to Build in Tableau:
1. Drag a **Dimension** (e.g., `Category`) to the **Rows** shelf.
2. Drag a **Measure** (e.g., `Sales`) to the **Columns** shelf.
3. Tableau will automatically generate a horizontal bar chart.
4. Click the **Sort Descending** icon on the toolbar to order the bars from highest to lowest sales.
5. *Enhancement:* Drag another Dimension (e.g., `Region`) onto the **Color** property in the Marks card to create a Stacked Bar Chart.

---

## 3. Line Charts (Time-Series Data)
Line charts are used exclusively to show trends over time.

### Theoretical Best Practices:
* **Don't skip time periods:** Ensure your timeline is continuous. If there is missing data, let the line break or interpolate, but do not compress the X-axis.
* **Keep it clean:** Avoid plotting more than 4-5 lines on a single chart to prevent the "spaghetti graph" effect.

### How to Build in Tableau:
1. Drag a Date **Dimension** (e.g., `Order Date`) to the **Columns** shelf.
2. Right-click the Date pill and change it from *Discrete* (blue) to *Continuous* (green) if you want a fluid timeline (e.g., continuous Months).
3. Drag a **Measure** (e.g., `Profit`) to the **Rows** shelf.
4. Tableau will default to a Line chart in the Marks card dropdown.
5. *Enhancement:* Drag `Category` to the **Color** mark to see separate trend lines for each category.

---

## 4. Scatter Plots (Relationships & Correlation)
Scatter plots are essential for identifying correlations, clusters, and outliers between *two numerical variables*.

### Theoretical Best Practices:
* **Look for patterns:** Is the trend positive, negative, or non-existent? 
* **Beware of overplotting:** If you have thousands of dots, use opacity (transparency) on your marks so overlapping points appear darker.

### How to Build in Tableau:
1. Drag a **Measure** (e.g., `Sales`) to the **Columns** shelf (X-axis).
2. Drag another **Measure** (e.g., `Profit`) to the **Rows** shelf (Y-axis).
    * *Note: Tableau will initially show just ONE dot, representing the sum of all sales vs. all profit.*
3. To break this single dot into individual data points, drag a granular **Dimension** (e.g., `Customer Name` or `Order ID`) onto the **Detail** property in the Marks card.
4. *Enhancement:* Drag `Discount` to the **Size** mark and `Region` to the **Color** mark to add third and fourth dimensions to your analysis.

---

## 5. Maps (Geospatial Analysis)
Tableau is highly effective at mapping geographic data (Countries, States, Cities, Postal Codes).

### Theoretical Best Practices:
* **Filled Maps (Choropleth):** Best for showing ratio or standardized data (e.g., Profit Margin % by State).
* **Symbol Maps:** Best for showing raw totals (e.g., Total Sales by City) using sized circles.

### How to Build in Tableau:
1. Ensure your geographic field (e.g., `State`) has a globe icon next to it in the Data pane. If not, right-click -> Geographic Role -> State/Province.
2. Double-click the `State` dimension. Tableau will automatically plot points on a map using generated Latitude (Rows) and Longitude (Columns).
3. **For a Symbol Map:** Drag a measure like `Sales` to the **Size** mark.
4. **For a Filled Map:** Change the dropdown in the Marks card from *Automatic* to *Map*, then drag `Profit` to the **Color** mark. 

---

## 6. Using Calculated Fields for Visualization Logic
Sometimes standard fields aren't enough, and you need to build logic directly into your visualizations. 

For example, you might want to color bars green if they hit a target, and red if they missed it. You can do this by creating a Calculated Field:

```sql
// KPI Color Calculation
IF SUM([Sales]) >= 100000 THEN 'Target Met'
ELSE 'Target Missed'
END
```

* Drag this new calculated field onto the **Color** mark to dynamically style your visualization based on performance!
